
## Storage Systems
The hospital data platform relies on several storage technologies, each chosen for a specific role. A transactional database such as PostgreSQL or MySQL is responsible for routine operations, including patient details, treatment information, admissions, and billing records. This OLTP layer is tuned for strong consistency, low latency, and high throughput so that front line hospital activities can run smoothly in real time. Alongside it, a data lake (for example, AWS S3 or a Hadoop based system) stores raw and semi structured data, such as long term treatment histories and continuous ICU monitoring feeds from medical devices. Because it is inexpensive and highly scalable, the data lake is well suited for holding very large volumes of information in its native format and then feeding downstream analytics and machine learning workflows. On top of this, a data warehouse like Snowflake or Google BigQuery is used for structured analytical queries and reporting. Curated data is moved from the lake into the warehouse via ETL pipelines, enabling efficient queries for monthly performance dashboards, occupancy statistics, and department wise cost and revenue tracking. Additionally, a vector database such as Pinecone or FAISS stores embeddings generated from patient records and related clinical documents, enabling semantic retrieval. This allows clinicians to ask natural language questions and quickly surface relevant patient information as part of an AI driven clinical decision support tool.

## OLTP vs OLAP Boundary
The architecture maintains a clear separation between OLTP and OLAP workloads to protect performance and scalability. The OLTP side consists of the operational database where real time hospital tasks such as registrations, order entries, record updates, and billing are performed. Analytical processing begins once data is extracted from this operational layer and ingested into the analytical stack, starting with the data lake. There, data arrives in raw form and then goes through cleaning, transformation, and modelling before being loaded into the data warehouse for multidimensional analysis and reporting. In practice, the boundary between OLTP and OLAP lies at the ingestion pipeline: once data leaves the transactional system and is written to the lake or warehouse, it is considered part of the analytical environment. This separation ensures that heavy analytical queries do not slow down mission critical hospital systems while still enabling rich analytics and decision support.

## Trade offs
A major trade off in this design is balancing real time insight with architectural complexity. Streaming ICU signals through technologies like Kafka enables immediate alerts and live dashboards but introduces extra components to deploy, monitor, and secure, increasing operational overhead. To manage this, a hybrid strategy is used: life critical vitals are processed in real time, while less urgent measurements are batched and written to the data lake for later analysis. This keeps the streaming layer focused on high value events and limits unnecessary load. Using managed cloud services—such as hosted Kafka and serverless ETL tools—further reduces the burden of infrastructure management, though at somewhat higher ongoing cost, and improves reliability and scalability in a healthcare setting.
