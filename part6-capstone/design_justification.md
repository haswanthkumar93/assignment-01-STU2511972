### Storage Systems

The hospital data platform relies on multiple storage technologies, each chosen to suit a particular type of workload. A transactional database such as PostgreSQL or MySQL handles day‑to‑day operations, including patient details, treatments, admissions, and billing events. This OLTP system is tuned for real‑time use, providing strong consistency and very fast reads and writes, which is essential for smooth clinical and administrative workflows. Alongside this, a data lake (for example, AWS S3 or a Hadoop‑based store) keeps large amounts of raw and semi‑structured information, such as long‑term treatment histories and continuous ICU device streams. Because a data lake scales cheaply and can hold data in its original format, it becomes the main layer for long‑term storage, advanced processing, and machine learning pipelines. On top of that, a data warehouse like Snowflake or BigQuery is used for structured analytics, where curated data is loaded via ETL processes from the data lake. This warehouse supports fast, complex queries for management reports, including monthly KPIs, bed usage patterns, and financial metrics by department. In addition, a vector database such as Pinecone or FAISS stores embeddings generated from patient records and related documents, enabling semantic search over clinical information. With this layer, clinicians can phrase questions in natural language and receive contextually relevant results, forming the backbone of an AI‑driven decision support tool. 

### OLTP vs OLAP Boundary

The design draws a clear line between operational (OLTP) workloads and analytical (OLAP) workloads to keep both reliable and fast. The OLTP side is the live hospital system where registrations, chart updates, orders, and billing transactions occur and where low latency and high concurrency are most important. Analytical processing starts once data is extracted from this operational database, typically in scheduled or streaming ingestion jobs that write into the data lake. Data first lands in the lake in unprocessed form, and then transformation steps clean, enrich, and model it before loading it into the data warehouse for multi‑dimensional analysis. In practice, the OLTP/OLAP boundary is at this ingestion layer: as soon as records leave the transactional store and arrive in the lake or warehouse, they are considered part of the analytics environment. This separation ensures that heavy reporting and data science queries do not slow down frontline hospital systems, while still giving analysts and leaders timely, high‑quality information.

### Trade‑offs
A key trade‑off in this setup involves the desire for real‑time processing versus the added complexity of supporting it. Capturing ICU vitals continuously through streaming tools such as Kafka provides near‑instant insight but also brings extra infrastructure to deploy, monitor, and secure, along with more potential failure points. To handle this, the architecture uses a hybrid approach: data points that affect immediate patient safety are processed as they arrive, powering alerts and live dashboards, while non‑urgent metrics are batched and sent later into the data lake. This strategy keeps the streaming layer focused on truly critical signals and avoids overloading it with low‑priority information. Another way to reduce operational burden is to rely on managed cloud offerings—such as hosted Kafka clusters or serverless ETL services—that handle scaling, patching, and failover automatically. Although these managed options can cost more than self‑managed systems, they typically offer better reliability, elasticity, and maintainability, which is valuable in a hospital setting. Overall, the architecture aims to strike a balance between speed, robustness, and complexity by combining real‑time and batch flows and mapping each use case to the most appropriate storage layer.
